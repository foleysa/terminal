Why This Category Is Different
Geography Is Destiny
Concrete sets in 60-90 minutes. It cannot be shipped long distances. Your ready-mix supplier options are limited to plants within a 20-30 mile radius of your project site. Aggregates (sand, gravel, crushed stone) are heavy and low-value — freight cost exceeds product cost beyond 30-50 miles. This creates natural geographic monopolies. In many markets, one or two producers control 70-80% of concrete and aggregate supply within your delivery radius. Unlike steel or chemicals, you cannot source from a distant competitor to create leverage. Your leverage comes from project timing, volume commitment, and understanding the producer's capacity utilization.
IIJA Demand Surge
The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA) is pumping $1.2 trillion into roads, bridges, water systems, broadband, and energy infrastructure through 2030. This creates sustained demand for concrete, aggregates, cement, rebar, and structural steel. Producers have pricing power because demand exceeds capacity in many regions. If you have construction projects in the next 2-3 years, lock in material pricing early — the IIJA demand wave will keep prices elevated through the decade.

Local Market Dynamics — Your Leverage Depends on Your LocationFSA ANALYSIS · USGS
The 30-Mile Rule
For ready-mix concrete and aggregates, your market is a circle with a 20-30 mile radius around your project site. Every ready-mix plant and aggregate quarry within that circle is a potential supplier. Every one outside it is not — the physics of concrete setting time and the economics of aggregate freight make long-distance sourcing impossible. Before negotiating, map every producer within your radius. If there are 4+ ready-mix plants, you have competition. If there are 1-2, you have a local monopoly and your leverage is limited to project timing and volume.
How to Find Leverage in a Local Market
LEVERAGE 1 — CAPACITY UTILIZATION
Ready-mix plants have fixed capacity (number of trucks, plant throughput). When utilization is high (spring/summer construction season), producers have no incentive to discount. When utilization drops (winter, recession, between projects), they need volume to cover fixed costs. Time your pours for off-peak periods when possible. A winter pour on a heated slab costs 5-10% more in heating but 10-20% less in concrete pricing.
LEVERAGE 2 — VOLUME COMMITMENT
A 500-yard project is a one-day job for a ready-mix plant. A 50,000-yard project is 6 months of steady volume. Producers discount for volume because it fills their capacity and reduces their per-unit fixed cost. On large projects (10,000+ yards), negotiate a per-yard price that includes delivery, and lock it for the project duration with a CPI cap on escalation.
LEVERAGE 3 — MULTI-PROJECT AGGREGATION
If you have 5 projects across a metro area over 12 months totaling 25,000 yards, aggregate them into a single master supply agreement. The producer sees 25,000 yards of committed volume instead of 5 separate small orders. You get the volume discount, scheduling priority, and price lock that a single 5,000-yard project would never command.
Regional Pricing Variation
Concrete Pricing Is Hyper-Local
Ready-mix concrete pricing varies 30-50% between metro areas depending on: number of competing plants, local aggregate availability, cement plant proximity, labor costs, and construction demand. A 4,000 PSI mix costs $130-160/yard in competitive markets (Houston, DFW) and $180-220/yard in constrained markets (NYC metro, Bay Area). There is no national concrete price — only local ones. Use USGS cement production data and PCA (Portland Cement Association) regional reports to understand your local market dynamics.
Aggregate Scarcity — The Hidden Constraint
Aggregate (sand, gravel, crushed stone) is the most consumed natural resource on earth after water. In many urban/suburban areas, quarry permits are impossible to obtain due to zoning, environmental, and NIMBY opposition. When local quarries close and are not replaced, aggregate must be hauled from farther away — driving up cost for every concrete and asphalt project in the region. Check your state's USGS mineral survey for aggregate production trends. A region losing quarries is a region where construction costs will only increase.
Cement — Import Dependency
The US imports 15-20% of its cement (primarily from Canada, Turkey, Greece, China). Cement imports are concentrated in coastal markets where import terminals provide an alternative to domestic producers. Inland markets are more dependent on domestic producers (who know it). If your project is near a port with a cement import terminal, you have an additional competitive lever that inland projects do not.

Buy America / Build America Buy America (BABA) ComplianceFHWA · EPA · OMB
If Your Project Uses Federal Funds, BABA Applies
The Build America, Buy America Act (BABA, part of IIJA) requires that all iron, steel, manufactured products, and construction materials used in federally funded infrastructure projects be produced in the United States. This is broader than the old Buy America (BAA) provisions — BABA covers construction materials (cement, aggregates, glass, fiber optic cable, etc.) that were previously exempt. Every IIJA-funded project must comply. Waivers exist but are narrow and temporary. Non-compliance means replacing materials at your cost and potentially losing federal funding.
What Must Be Domestic
IRON & STEEL — ALL PROCESSES DOMESTIC
All manufacturing processes for iron and steel must occur in the US: melting, rolling, forming, cutting, welding, galvanizing, coating, and fabricating. Rebar must be melted AND fabricated domestically. Structural steel must be melted AND formed domestically. A rebar fabricator using imported raw steel does NOT comply. The steel itself must be melted in a US furnace.
MANUFACTURED PRODUCTS — FINAL ASSEMBLY + 55% DOMESTIC
Manufactured products (pumps, valves, electrical equipment, HVAC) must be manufactured in the US, with more than 55% of the cost of components mined, produced, or manufactured domestically. This 55% threshold is being phased in — check current OMB guidance for the applicable percentage. Products with significant foreign content may fail the test.
CONSTRUCTION MATERIALS — NEW UNDER BABA
BABA newly covers: cement, aggregates, glass (including optical fiber), fiber optic cable (including drop cable), plastic and polymer-based products, drywall, lumber, and composite building materials. All manufacturing processes must occur in the US. This is the biggest change from old BAA — construction materials were previously exempt. Verify domestic origin for every construction material on a federally funded project.
Compliance Strategy
STEP 1 — IDENTIFY FEDERAL FUNDING
Determine if your project uses ANY federal funding: IIJA formula funds (highways, bridges, water), FHWA, EPA SRF (State Revolving Fund), DOE, USDA, or FEMA. Even partial federal funding triggers BABA for the ENTIRE project — not just the federally funded portion. If any dollar is federal, every material must comply.
STEP 2 — CERTIFY AT PROCUREMENT
Get BABA compliance certifications from every material supplier BEFORE placing orders. Require: country of melting (iron/steel), country of manufacturing (products), domestic content percentage (manufactured products), and a signed certification letter. Do not accept verbal assurances. Put certification requirements in every PO and subcontract.
STEP 3 — DOCUMENT EVERYTHING
Maintain a BABA compliance file for every material on the project. Include: supplier certifications, mill test reports (for steel — showing US mill), buy-out logs showing domestic sourcing, and any waiver requests. Federal auditors can request this documentation years after project completion. If you cannot prove compliance, you may have to repay federal funds.
Waivers
Three waiver types: (1) Non-availability: domestic product not available in sufficient quantity/quality. (2) Unreasonable cost: domestic product costs 25%+ more than foreign alternative. (3) Public interest: compliance would be inconsistent with public interest. Waivers are granted by the federal agency (FHWA, EPA, etc.) and require a formal application. The process takes 30-120 days. Plan waiver requests early — they are not a last-minute option.

If you have multiple projects in the same metro area over the next 12 months, aggregate them into a single master supply agreement for concrete and aggregates. A 25,000-yard commitment gets dramatically better pricing than five 5,000-yard projects bid separately. The producer sees steady volume to fill their capacity — you get the volume discount and scheduling priority.
AGGREGATION · VOLUME · MASTER AGREEMENT
STRATEGIC — NEXT QUARTER
Evaluate supplementary cementitious materials (SCMs) — fly ash, slag cement, silica fume — as partial cement replacements in concrete mixes. SCMs reduce cement content 15-40% per yard, lower CO2 emissions (cement is 8% of global CO2), reduce material cost, and often improve concrete durability. Most ready-mix producers can supply SCM blends. Specify SCM allowances in your mix designs.
SCM · FLY ASH · SLAG · SUSTAINABILITY
STRATEGIC — NEXT QUARTER
For large projects, evaluate precast concrete vs cast-in-place. Precast (manufactured in a controlled factory, delivered to site) reduces on-site labor, eliminates weather delays, improves quality consistency, and can be faster to install. Cost premium: 5-15% for material but often offset by reduced labor and schedule. Oldcastle Infrastructure (CRH), Rinker/Quikrete, and regional precasters all offer precast alternatives.
PRECAST · LABOR · SCHEDULE · QUALITY
MONITOR
