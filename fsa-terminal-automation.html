Key DependenciesANALYSIS
Software Licensing — The Recurring Cost Trap
Automation OEMs are shifting from perpetual licenses to annual subscriptions. Rockwell's FactoryTalk, Siemens TIA Portal, and Schneider's EcoStruxure all moving to subscription models. What was a one-time capital expense is becoming an escalating annual operating expense. Factor licensing into TCO over the equipment lifecycle — not just the purchase year.
Integration Labor — The Hidden 30-50%
The hardware is 40-50% of total automation project cost. Integration, programming, commissioning, and validation are the other 50-60%. Controls engineers are in critical shortage — BLS shows 7.2% wage growth. System integrators are booked 3-6 months out. The labor constraint, not the hardware, is what delays most automation projects.
Semiconductor Allocation — Improved But Not Normal
Chip supply has improved from the 2021-2023 crisis but power semiconductors (IGBTs, MOSFETs) used in VFDs and drives remain constrained. Lead times have come down from 52+ weeks to 12-20 weeks — better, but still well above the pre-COVID 4-6 week norm. Budget for extended lead times through 2027.

Platform Lock-In Analysis — The Most Expensive Decision in AutomationFSA ANALYSIS
The Problem
Choosing an automation platform is a 15-20 year commitment. PLCs, programming software, I/O, drives, HMIs, and SCADA are all designed to work within one ecosystem. Rockwell's Allen-Bradley PLCs use Studio 5000 for programming, FactoryTalk for SCADA, PowerFlex for drives — all proprietary, all interoperable within the ecosystem, all incompatible with competitors. Switching from Rockwell to Siemens means replacing hardware, rewriting every program, retraining every technician, and revalidating every process. Cost: $500K-5M+ per facility depending on size.
Lock-In Dimensions
PROGRAMMING LANGUAGE
Each OEM uses proprietary extensions to IEC 61131-3 standard. Rockwell's Studio 5000 code won't run on Siemens TIA Portal. Programs must be completely rewritten to switch platforms. This is the single biggest switching cost.
I/O & NETWORKING
Rockwell uses EtherNet/IP, Siemens uses PROFINET, Mitsubishi uses CC-Link. Different industrial networks, different I/O modules, different wiring. The physical infrastructure is platform-specific.
TECHNICIAN TRAINING
Your maintenance electricians and controls engineers know ONE platform deeply. A Rockwell shop can troubleshoot Allen-Bradley in their sleep. Switch to Siemens and your entire team needs retraining — 6-12 months to proficiency.
SPARE PARTS INVENTORY
Your MRO storeroom stocks PLCs, I/O cards, drives, and HMIs for your installed platform. Switching means a complete spare parts refresh — or maintaining two parallel inventories during transition.
How to Manage Lock-In
STRATEGY 1 — STANDARDIZE BUT NEGOTIATE HARD
Accept the lock-in but use it as leverage. "We're committing our entire automation standard to your platform. In return, we need enterprise pricing, annual price caps, priority allocation, and a dedicated support engineer." Volume commitment = negotiating power.
STRATEGY 2 — DUAL STANDARD
Run two platforms (e.g., Rockwell for discrete, Siemens for process). Higher complexity but creates competitive tension. Each OEM knows the other is in your plants. Pricing stays competitive because neither has a monopoly.
STRATEGY 3 — OPEN STANDARDS WHERE POSSIBLE
OPC-UA, MQTT, and open industrial Ethernet are reducing lock-in at the communication layer. For new projects, spec OPC-UA compatibility for all SCADA/HMI and data collection. This doesn't eliminate PLC lock-in but prevents the software layer from being locked too.
STRATEGY 4 — COBOT EXCEPTION
Collaborative robots (Universal Robots, FANUC CRX, ABB GoFa) are ecosystem-agnostic. They integrate with any PLC platform via standard I/O. Use cobots to add automation capability without deepening your PLC ecosystem lock-in.

Japan & Germany Advantage
The major automation OEMs (Siemens/Germany, FANUC/Omron/Mitsubishi/Japan, ABB/Switzerland, Rockwell/US) all manufacture outside China. Section 301 tariffs primarily affect Chinese-brand PLCs (Inovance, Delta Electronics) and Chinese-origin components inside global OEM products. For major-brand automation, the tariff exposure is in the component supply chain — not the finished product.
Chinese Automation — Price vs. Tariff
Chinese automation brands (Inovance, Delta, Kinco) offer PLCs and VFDs at 40-60% below Rockwell/Siemens list price. Even with 25% Section 301 tariff, they're still cheaper. But the ecosystem lock-in works in reverse — Chinese PLCs use Chinese programming software, Chinese I/O, and Chinese support. The total cost of ownership including integration, support, and spare parts may not justify the savings for critical applications.
