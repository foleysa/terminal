Key DependenciesANALYSIS
Copper — 25% of Motor Cost
Every motor, generator, and transformer uses copper windings. LME inventories at 15-year low. Premium motors (IE4/IE5) use MORE copper than standard efficiency motors for better thermal performance. The efficiency mandate is increasing your copper exposure.
Rare Earth Magnets — China Dominance
Permanent magnet motors (PM/BLDC) use neodymium magnets. China controls 60%+ of rare earth production and 90% of processing. Section 301 tariff applies. Any escalation in US-China tensions directly threatens PM motor supply. Induction motors (no rare earths) are the hedge.
Lead Times — Structural, Not Temporary
16-24 week lead times for standard motors. Custom engineered pumps and compressors: 26-40 weeks. This isn't a post-COVID backlog — it's structural. Skilled machinist and winder labor is the bottleneck, and it's not getting better. Plan 6-9 months ahead for any critical rotating equipment.

Rebuild vs Replace EconomicsFSA ANALYSIS
Why This Matters
With motor lead times at 16-24 weeks and copper driving new equipment prices up 8-15%, the rebuild-vs-replace decision has fundamentally changed. Rebuilding a motor at 40-60% of replacement cost with a 4-6 week turnaround is often the better business decision — especially for critical path equipment.
When to Rebuild
REBUILD
Motor is less than 20 years old and frame/core are intact
REBUILD
Failure is winding-only (rewind at 30-40% of replacement cost)
REBUILD
Equipment is critical path and 24-week lead time for new is unacceptable
REBUILD
Original motor is oversized and a rewind + right-sizing improves efficiency
REBUILD
Specialty frame/voltage not readily available as new (medium voltage, explosion-proof)
When to Replace
REPLACE
Motor has been rewound 2+ times already (diminishing efficiency returns)
REPLACE
DOE efficiency mandate requires IE4+ and rebuild can't meet the standard
REPLACE
Energy cost savings from IE4/IE5 motor pay back the premium in under 2 years
REPLACE
Frame/core damage (bearing journal wear, cracked frame, core degradation)
REPLACE
Planned replacement with spare — keep rebuilt motor as emergency backup
TCO Calculation
For a 200HP motor running 8,000 hours/year at $0.08/kWh: the energy cost is ~$95,000/year. Replacing a standard efficiency motor with IE4 saves 3-5% = $2,850-4,750/year. New IE4 200HP motor costs ~$8,000. Payback: 2-3 years. But if the existing motor is only 5 years old and needs a $3,200 rewind — rebuild it, run it for 5 more years, then replace with IE5 when the next generation is available.
Source: DOE Motor Systems Tip Sheets, EASA rewind guidelines, FSA TCO analysis

Tariff Arbitrage — Motors
Chinese-origin motors face 27.4% combined duty. EU (Siemens), Swiss (ABB), Brazilian (WEG), and Japanese (Nidec) motors enter at 2.4% MFN. WEG (Brazil) is the fastest-growing motor supplier in North America with a US factory in Minneapolis and aggressive pricing. For NEMA-frame commodity motors, WEG is often 15-20% cheaper than ABB/Siemens before you even factor in the tariff differential on the China alternatives.
Rare Earth Dependency
Permanent magnet motors use neodymium magnets — China controls 60% of rare earth mining and 90% of processing. Section 301 adds 25% tariff on rare earth magnets. If you spec PM motors, your magnet supply runs through China regardless of where the motor is assembled. Induction motors avoid this dependency entirely.

CONCENTRATION · OEM LOCK-IN · EXHIBIT 21
STRATEGIC — 30 DAYS
Establish a relationship with a qualified motor rewind shop. When a critical motor fails, the choice between a 4-week rebuild and a 24-week new motor is the difference between a brief outage and a quarter-long production loss. Pre-qualify the shop before you need them.
REBUILD · EMERGENCY RESPONSE · MRO
STRATEGIC — NEXT QUARTER
Run a motor census on your top 20 energy-consuming motors. Identify units running at less than 60% load (oversized) and units older than 15 years (efficiency degradation). These are your replacement candidates. IE4 motors on high-runtime equipment pay back in 2-3 years at current energy prices.
EFFICIENCY · DOE · MOTOR CENSUS
STRATEGIC — NEXT QUARTER
If you spec permanent magnet motors, evaluate whether induction alternatives can meet the application requirements. PM motors have superior efficiency but create a rare earth dependency on China. Induction motors avoid this entirely. The efficiency gap is narrowing — IE4 induction motors are close to PM performance in many applications.
RARE EARTH · PM vs INDUCTION · RISK
MONITOR
DOE efficiency mandates are tightening. IE3 is the current US minimum. IE4 will likely become mandatory within 2-3 years. Start transitioning your standard motor specifications to IE4 now — the price premium is 10-15% but the energy payback is real, and you'll avoid a compliance scramble when the mandate takes effect.
