The Fourth Utility Nobody Manages
Most Expensive Utility Per Unit of Energy
Compressed air costs $0.18-0.30 per 1,000 cubic feet — primarily electricity. Only 10-15% of electrical energy becomes useful compressed air; the rest is heat. A 100 HP compressor running 8,000 hours/year costs $50,000-70,000 in electricity alone. Yet most plants don't meter compressed air, don't track cost per CFM, and don't manage it as a utility.
The Procurement Opportunity
A typical mid-size plant spends $200K-800K/year on compressed air (electricity + maintenance + equipment). Most can reduce by 20-35% through three actions: fix leaks (10-15% savings), add VFD control (10-20%), and reduce pressure (2% per 1 PSI drop). On $500K annual cost, that is $100K-175K/year — without buying from a new supplier.
Urgent ActionsTHIS WEEK
URGENT — FREE MONEY
Schedule an ultrasonic leak survey. Every plant has leaks — typically 25-30% of total production is lost. An ultrasonic detector ($3K-5K to buy, or $2K-4K for a contracted survey) identifies leaks you cannot hear over plant noise. Fixing leaks is the single highest-ROI energy project in manufacturing. Payback: days to weeks.

Lifecycle Cost — The 70/10/20 RuleDOE · CAGI
Purchase Price Is Almost Irrelevant
Over 10 years: 70% is electricity, 10% is equipment, 20% is maintenance. A $50K compressor consumes $350K-500K in electricity over its life. A cheaper compressor that is 5% less efficient costs $17K-25K more in energy over 10 years. Always buy on specific power (kW/100 CFM), not sticker price.
ELECTRICITY — 70%
Tracks EIA regional rates. 100 HP compressor draws ~75 kW. At $0.10/kWh x 8,000 hrs = $60K/yr. At $0.15/kWh = $90K/yr. Regional electricity cost is the #1 variable.
MAINTENANCE — 20%
Oil, filters, separators, coolant, belts, valve kits. OEM maintenance contracts typically 25-40% above independent service. Third-party consumables available at 30-50% savings for most brands.
EQUIPMENT — 10%
Rotary screw: $15K-150K. Centrifugal: $200K-1M+. Reciprocating: $5K-50K. The equipment is the cheapest part. Spec on specific power, not purchase price.
CAGI Data Sheets — Your WeaponCAGI
Specific Power = kW per 100 CFM
This is the ONLY metric for comparing compressors. 18 kW/100 CFM is 10% more efficient than 20 kW/100 CFM. Over 10 years at 8,000 hrs/yr and $0.10/kWh, that 2 kW difference on a 500 CFM compressor costs $80,000 in electricity. CAGI publishes verified performance sheets for all major models — use them. Free at cagi.org.
Air as a Service (CaaS)
Atlas Copco, Kaeser, and independents offer compressed air as a service — they own the compressor, maintain it, charge you per CFM delivered. Converts CAPEX to OPEX, transfers maintenance to OEM, aligns incentives (they want efficiency because they pay the electric bill). Worth evaluating for new installations or aging equipment replacements.

Energy Optimization & Leak Audit — Where The Real Money IsDOE · CAGI · EIA
You Are Paying to Compress Air That Leaks Into the Ceiling
DOE estimates 25-30% of compressed air in a typical plant is lost through leaks. On a 1,000 CFM system, that is 250-300 CFM you pay to produce and never use. At $0.25/1,000 CFM, a 1,000 CFM system with 25% leaks wastes $4,000-6,000/month in electricity. Leaks are in fittings, quick-connects, hoses, drain valves, pipe joints. You cannot hear most over plant noise — you need an ultrasonic detector.
The Three Energy Plays
PLAY 1 — LEAK DETECTION & REPAIR (10-15% savings)
Buy a Fluke ii900 or SDT 270 ultrasonic detector ($3K-5K) or hire an auditor ($2K-4K). Tag every leak. Fix in priority order by estimated CFM loss. Most leaks: tighten a fitting, replace a coupling, swap a valve. Repeat quarterly — leaks come back. Payback measured in days.
PLAY 2 — VFD COMPRESSOR (15-35% savings)
Fixed-speed compressors run full power regardless of demand. VFD matches motor speed to actual demand. If demand fluctuates (most plants), VFD saves 15-35%. New VFD compressors cost 15-25% more upfront. Utility rebates often cover 30-50%. Payback: 12-24 months.
PLAY 3 — PRESSURE OPTIMIZATION (1% per 2 PSI)
Most plants run 20-30 PSI above actual need. Higher pressure = higher energy AND higher leak rates (proportional to pressure squared). Map actual requirements. Install point-of-use boosters for high-pressure applications. Reduce system pressure to match majority demand. Free savings.
Bonus: Heat Recovery
85-90% of Compressor Energy Becomes Heat
A 100 HP compressor rejects ~210,000 BTU/hr — enough to heat a 5,000 sq ft building. Heat recovery systems cost $5K-15K and pay back in 1-2 heating seasons in cold climates. Atlas Copco and Kaeser both offer integrated options.
Free DOE & Utility Resources
DOE Compressed Air Challenge: Free training for plant engineers. DOE Industrial Assessment Centers: Free energy audits for qualifying mid-size manufacturers (under 500 employees, under $100M revenue). Utility rebates: Most utilities offer $50-200/HP for VFD compressors and leak repair programs.
Source: DOE Compressed Air Challenge, CAGI, EIA, utility rebate databases
Tariff ExposureUSTR · ITC
Low Tariff Category
Major OEMs (Atlas Copco/Sweden, Kaeser/Germany, IR/US, Sullair/Hitachi with US manufacturing) produce outside China or domestically. Chinese compressors face 25% Section 301 but have minimal market share in industrial applications above 25 HP. The cost driver is electricity, not trade policy.
