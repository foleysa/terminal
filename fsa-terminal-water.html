Treatment Provider MarketANALYSIS
Same Lock-In as Cooling Towers
Water treatment providers (Nalco/Ecolab, ChemTreat/Danaher, Veolia, Solenis) bundle chemistry, service, and monitoring into single contracts. The provider controls your water chemistry and switching feels risky. The result: 20-30% overspend on chemicals with no competitive pressure. The fix is the same: unbundle, get independent testing, and run a competitive RFP.
Water Reuse — Growing Opportunity
Water scarcity, rising utility costs, and tightening discharge limits are making water reuse economically viable. Treating and recycling process water (membrane systems, advanced oxidation) can reduce water purchase costs 30-50% AND reduce discharge volume. The capital investment pays back in 2-4 years at current water/sewer rates in most metros.
Treatment Chemical PricingBLS · ICIS
CAUSTIC SODA (NaOH) — +14%
Primary pH adjustment chemical for wastewater. Produced via chlor-alkali process (electricity-intensive). Price tracks electricity + chlorine demand. Gulf Coast producers dominate. Freight is 15-20% of delivered cost due to weight.
CHLORINE — +8%
Disinfection standard. Co-produced with caustic soda (chlor-alkali). Price inversely correlated with caustic demand. Currently rising due to PVC demand (chlorine → vinyl chloride → PVC). Evaluate UV or ozone as chlorine alternatives for disinfection.
POLYMER FLOCCULANTS — +10%
Acrylamide-based polymers for solids separation. Petrochemical-derived — tracks crude oil. Specialty chemicals with OEM-spec lock-in risk (treatment providers specify their proprietary formulations). Generic equivalents exist at 20-30% savings.
SULFURIC ACID — +6%
pH reduction, metal precipitation. Byproduct of smelting and refinery operations. Supply tightens when refinery utilization drops. Currently adequate supply but crude-linked through refinery economics.
ACTIVATED CARBON — +4%
PFAS removal, taste/odor control. Demand surging due to PFAS MCL compliance — every water system needing PFAS treatment is buying GAC (granular activated carbon). Supply is adequate for now but expect tightening as MCL enforcement ramps up. Coal-based (domestic) vs coconut-based (imported) sources.
RO/UF MEMBRANES — +5%
Reverse osmosis and ultrafiltration membranes for water reuse, desalination, and advanced treatment. Dominated by Dow/DuPont (FilmTec), Toray, and Hydranautics (Nitto Denko). Membrane replacement is the major recurring cost — typically every 3-7 years. Proper pretreatment extends membrane life and reduces replacement frequency.
ION EXCHANGE RESIN — +7%
PFAS removal (emerging application), water softening, demineralization. Purolite (Ecolab), Dow, Lanxess produce the resin. PFAS-selective resins are premium priced but increasingly specified for compliance with the 4 ppt MCL.
Source: BLS PPI-Chemicals, ICIS caustic/chlorine, EPA, AWWA

PFAS Compliance — The Biggest Regulatory Event in 20 YearsEPA · FEDERAL REGISTER
What Happened
EPA finalized Maximum Contaminant Levels (MCLs) for six PFAS compounds in drinking water. PFOA and PFOS: 4 parts per trillion (ppt). PFNA, PFHxS, HFPO-DA (GenX): 10 ppt. Combined PFAS: Hazard Index of 1.0. These are enforceable limits. Every public water system must comply. Every industrial discharger upstream of a public water system is affected.
What It Means for Industrial Facilities
DISCHARGE PERMITS WILL TIGHTEN
If your facility discharges to a municipal sewer (indirect discharge) or surface water (direct discharge), your NPDES or pretreatment permit will eventually include PFAS limits. Municipal water systems facing the 4 ppt MCL will push limits upstream to industrial contributors. Start testing your effluent for PFAS now — before the permit requires it.
PFAS SOURCES IN YOUR FACILITY
Common industrial PFAS sources: firefighting foam (AFFF), non-stick coatings/mold release agents, surfactants in cleaning chemicals, waterproofing treatments, chrome plating chemicals, and some hydraulic fluids. Audit every chemical input for PFAS content. Your SDS (Safety Data Sheet) may not disclose PFAS — request specific PFAS declarations from suppliers.
TREATMENT OPTIONS
GAC (Granular Activated Carbon): Most common, proven, moderate cost. Requires periodic media replacement.
Ion Exchange (IX): Higher removal efficiency, selective resins available. Higher operating cost.
High-Pressure Membranes (NF/RO): Highest removal but creates concentrated waste stream.
Advanced Oxidation: Emerging — can destroy PFAS rather than just removing it.
Compliance Timeline
2024 — MCL FINALIZED
EPA published final rule. Legal challenges ongoing but compliance timeline is ticking regardless.
2025-2026 — MONITORING
Public water systems must begin monitoring for PFAS. Industrial dischargers should begin voluntary monitoring to understand their baseline.
2027-2029 — COMPLIANCE DEADLINE
Public water systems must meet MCLs. Pretreatment programs will push limits to industrial contributors. If your facility contributes PFAS to a municipal sewer, expect enforceable limits in your next permit renewal.
STATE ACTION — ALREADY AHEAD
Michigan, New Jersey, New Hampshire, Vermont, and other states already have PFAS drinking water standards — some stricter than EPA's MCL. If you operate in these states, compliance is already required. Check your state EPA website for state-specific PFAS standards.
Cost Estimate
PFAS treatment capital costs range from $50K (small GAC system for a single discharge point) to $5M+ (large industrial facility with multiple sources). Operating costs (media replacement, monitoring, disposal) add 15-25% annually. Budget for this in your 2026-2028 capital plan — it's not optional.
Source: EPA Final PFAS MCL Rule (89 FR 32532), state PFAS programs, AWWA
Tariff Exposure — Water TreatmentUSTR · ITC
Low Direct Tariff Exposure
Water treatment chemicals (caustic soda, chlorine, polymers, sulfuric acid) are overwhelmingly produced domestically by Gulf Coast chemical plants. Treatment equipment (membranes, UV systems, monitoring instruments) has moderate China exposure on components but most major equipment OEMs (Xylem, Veolia, Ecolab) manufacture or assemble in the US or USMCA countries. The primary cost drivers are energy (electricity for chemical production), freight (heavy chemicals are expensive to ship), and regulatory compliance — not tariffs.
Activated Carbon — Watch
Coconut-shell activated carbon is imported (primarily Philippines, Indonesia, India). Coal-based GAC is domestic (Calgon Carbon/Kuraray, Cabot Norit). As PFAS treatment demand surges, coconut-shell GAC imports may face supply constraints. Domestic coal-based GAC avoids import dependency. Specify coal-based for PFAS compliance projects unless coconut-shell is specifically required for the application.

Strategic Sourcing Actions — Water & WastewaterFSA INTELLIGENCE
CRITICAL — ACT THIS MONTH
Test your facility's effluent for PFAS (PFOA, PFOS, and the full EPA Method 533 panel). Don't wait for your discharge permit to require it. Knowing your baseline gives you time to identify sources, evaluate treatment options, and budget for compliance. A single PFAS test panel costs $300-500. The cost of non-compliance is orders of magnitude higher.
PFAS · TESTING · BASELINE
URGENT — ACT THIS WEEK
Audit every chemical input for PFAS content. Request PFAS-specific declarations from your chemical suppliers — standard SDS may not disclose PFAS. Firefighting foam (AFFF), surfactants, mold release agents, and chrome plating chemicals are the most common sources. Identify and eliminate PFAS inputs before investing in end-of-pipe treatment.
PFAS · AUDIT · CHEMICAL INPUTS

Map your Ecolab/Nalco exposure across all water-related categories: boiler water, cooling tower, process water, wastewater. Add in any Ecolab pest, food safety, or janitorial services. Total Ecolab spend across the enterprise gives you leverage that no single plant or category team has alone.

Reduces water purchase, reduces discharge volume, and reduces compliance risk simultaneously.
